Sleep Apnea: Signs and Treatment Options

May 20, 2026The Better Lungs Clinic

Learn the signs of sleep apnea, why snoring with daytime tiredness matters, and how sleep studies and CPAP support can help.

What sleep apnea means

Sleep apnea is a condition where breathing repeatedly pauses or becomes shallow during sleep. It is often linked with loud snoring, choking or gasping at night, restless sleep, morning headache, dry mouth, and daytime tiredness.

Many people think snoring is harmless, but snoring with pauses in breathing, poor sleep quality, or daytime sleepiness can affect oxygen levels, heart health, blood pressure, mood, and concentration.

Signs that need evaluation

Warning signs include loud habitual snoring, witnessed breathing pauses, waking up choking, excessive daytime sleepiness, morning headaches, poor concentration, irritability, uncontrolled blood pressure, and feeling unrefreshed despite enough hours in bed.

Sleep apnea can affect people with weight gain, neck crowding, nasal blockage, alcohol use, smoking, hypothyroid concerns, or family history, but it can also occur in people who do not fit the common stereotype.

How sleep apnea is diagnosed

A pulmonology visit reviews sleep symptoms, medical history, breathing concerns, oxygen issues, and risk factors. A sleep study may be advised to measure breathing pauses, oxygen changes, sleep disruption, and severity.

The test results help decide whether lifestyle changes, CPAP or BIPAP support, nasal care, weight management, or other specialist input may be needed. Treatment is personalized to severity and comfort.

Why treatment helps

Treating sleep apnea can improve daytime alertness, sleep quality, morning headaches, snoring concerns, and long-term health risks. For many patients, the biggest challenge is getting used to therapy, so follow-up support matters.

The Better Lungs Clinic supports sleep apnea screening, sleep study guidance, CPAP or BIPAP discussion, and respiratory follow-up for patients with snoring, sleepiness, and breathing concerns.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is loud snoring always sleep apnea?

No. Snoring can happen without sleep apnea, but loud snoring with choking, breathing pauses, or daytime sleepiness should be evaluated.

What is a sleep study?

A sleep study records breathing, oxygen changes, and sleep-related events to help diagnose sleep apnea and assess severity.

Does every sleep apnea patient need CPAP?

Not always. Treatment depends on severity, symptoms, anatomy, risk factors, and test results. CPAP or BIPAP may be advised when appropriate.
